The Effect of Social Protection on Health

Some Background

As advanced capitalist countries increasingly lean towards complete privatisation, there's been a significant surge in the cost of living. This trend raises concerns about the potential negative effects of privatising healthcare on individuals with lower incomes, particularly as access to essential services may become increasingly unaffordable or limited for marginalized populations.

Additionally, the transition to privatisation could worsen the already present inequalities in healthcare access and outcomes, placing a disproportionate burden on marginalised communities that heavily depend on public healthcare services. In the absence of sufficient safeguards or provisions, privatisation may further widen the healthcare disparity between different socioeconomic groups, thereby compromising the fundamental principles of fairness and societal equality.

The Goal

Our goal is to determine whether there is validity to the notion that a decrease in socialised healthcare impacts citizens and the implications of privatisation on the healthcare industry. To achieve this, data regarding the health status of low-income earners across OECD nations will be collected and compared to that of high-income earners through a series of visualisations.

Given that low-income earners are more reliant on publicly-funded healthcare systems, a conclusion can be drawn on whether low-income individuals in heavily privatised nations experience health-related repercussions.
